# Treat the Disease, Not the Symptom

## Purpose

Help startup leaders diagnose what is really going wrong behind messy initiatives, morale issues, strategy drift, launches, rebrands, and second-product complexity.

This skill uses operator pattern recognition to look past the stated problem, identify the deeper operating disease, expose hidden work, and recommend the smallest useful intervention.

The medical metaphor should stay light. Use it for useful section labels, not gimmicks.

## Context Check

Before using this skill:

- Identify the presenting symptom: what the team says is wrong.
- Identify the initiative, function, team, or company context.
- Clarify the audience: private advisor, CEO/founder, or leadership team.
- Gather available evidence, such as timelines, roadmaps, meeting notes, customer feedback, support load, org charts, decision logs, or launch plans.
- Separate what is observed from what is inferred.
- If evidence is thin, state that the diagnosis is preliminary.

## When To Use

Use this skill when:

- A project, launch, rebrand, migration, or second product feels simpler on paper than it will be in reality.
- Leaders are describing a symptom such as slow execution, poor morale, confused customers, missed deadlines, or lack of alignment.
- A team needs to understand hidden work, maintenance burden, decision fog, or second-order consequences before committing.
- The user wants candid operator judgment, not a generic root-cause template.

Do not use it for ordinary task planning, performance review, or shallow brainstorming.

## Inputs

Useful inputs include:

- Stated problem or leadership concern
- Project plan, launch plan, roadmap, or rebrand brief
- Timeline, staffing, and ownership assumptions
- Customer, sales, support, or implementation feedback
- Known constraints and non-negotiables
- Prior attempts to solve the issue
- Any symptoms of morale, trust, churn, support load, or decision delay

## Severity Rating

Use one of these simple ratings:

| Rating | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Healthy | Normal execution risk. The plan may need discipline, but the stated problem is probably close to the real problem. |
| Watch | Early signs of hidden complexity, misalignment, or underestimated work. |
| At Risk | Meaningful under-scoping, ownership gaps, or second-order consequences are likely. |
| Going Sideways | The initiative is structurally likely to miss, drag, or create new problems. |
| Root Problem | The stated issue is mostly a symptom of a deeper company problem. |

## Pattern Library

Use these patterns as diagnostic prompts, not as labels to force onto every situation.

### Rebrand as Paint Job

- **Symptom:** The rebrand is treated as a design or messaging project.
- **Disease:** The company is underestimating identity migration, sales enablement, customer communication, product surfaces, legal review, internal alignment, and cleanup.
- **Telltale signs:** Tight timeline, unclear asset inventory, no owner for customer-facing rollout, no migration plan.
- **Treatment:** Expand the plan from brand work to operating rollout, with owners for surfaces, enablement, comms, and cleanup.

### Orphaned Second Product

- **Symptom:** The team wants to ship a second product before the first one is operationally mature.
- **Disease:** Leadership is creating a second operating model without accounting for support, maintenance, GTM clarity, roadmap split, and customer confusion.
- **Telltale signs:** Same team owns both products, unclear packaging, no support model, product one still has unresolved reliability or adoption issues.
- **Treatment:** Define the second product's owner, support path, maintenance budget, and GTM boundary before launch.

### Launch Without Operating Model

- **Symptom:** The launch plan focuses on date, messaging, and feature readiness.
- **Disease:** The company has not defined how the product will be sold, supported, measured, escalated, and improved after launch.
- **Telltale signs:** Launch checklist ends at release day, no clear support path, no training plan, no post-launch operating cadence.
- **Treatment:** Add the post-launch operating model before treating the launch as ready.

### Migration Iceberg

- **Symptom:** A migration is framed as moving users, data, pages, systems, or processes from old to new.
- **Disease:** The visible migration is only the tip of the work; edge cases, rollback, training, dependencies, customer confusion, and cleanup are hidden below the surface.
- **Telltale signs:** Few edge cases listed, no rollback owner, no inventory of impacted systems, confidence based on happy-path testing.
- **Treatment:** Inventory the full migration surface and separate must-move, can-later, and should-delete work.

### Roadmap Sprawl

- **Symptom:** The roadmap has too many important initiatives.
- **Disease:** Strategy is not making tradeoffs, so every stakeholder priority survives and execution becomes diluted.
- **Telltale signs:** Many top priorities, repeated slips, no explicit deprioritization, teams context-switching across unrelated goals.
- **Treatment:** Force tradeoffs around the few outcomes that matter most and name what will not happen now.

### Support Debt Transfer

- **Symptom:** A product or process change looks efficient for the building team.
- **Disease:** Complexity has been transferred to support, customer success, sales, implementation, operations, or customers.
- **Telltale signs:** Engineering scope shrinks while support scripts, exceptions, or manual work grow.
- **Treatment:** Count downstream load as part of project cost and redesign before declaring the work simpler.

### Decision-Rights Fog

- **Symptom:** Work is slow because people are waiting, rechecking, or relitigating decisions.
- **Disease:** Ownership and authority are unclear, so the organization keeps paying coordination tax.
- **Telltale signs:** Many approvers, unclear decider, meetings end without commitments, teams ask for alignment repeatedly.
- **Treatment:** Name the decider, input group, veto rights, escalation path, and decision deadline.

### Process Band-Aid

- **Symptom:** The company tries to fix communication, alignment, or handoff problems by adding process, such as a RACI, intake form, approval workflow, weekly sync, or status doc.
- **Disease:** The real issue is unclear ownership, mismatched team boundaries, weak decision rights, or low trust. Process may expose the confusion, but it will not fix the org design.
- **Telltale signs:** RACI before authority, more meetings without faster decisions, intake forms because ownership is disputed, process compliance becoming the work.
- **Treatment:** Clarify ownership, decision rights, team boundaries, and accountability first. Then add the lightest process needed to support that structure.

### Strategy Theater

- **Symptom:** Leaders talk about strategy, alignment, and priorities, but behavior does not change.
- **Disease:** The strategy does not force choices, concentrate effort, or rule out work.
- **Telltale signs:** Inspiring language, broad pillars, no resource shifts, every prior initiative still alive.
- **Treatment:** Convert strategy into tradeoffs, stopped work, concentrated resources, and coherent actions.

### Morale as Symptom

- **Symptom:** The team is described as unmotivated, negative, slow, or resistant.
- **Disease:** Morale may be reflecting unclear priorities, chronic thrash, low trust, overload, broken promises, or learned helplessness.
- **Telltale signs:** Repeated priority changes, private cynicism, missed commitments with no consequence, managers asking for enthusiasm instead of removing blockers.
- **Treatment:** Diagnose the operating cause before prescribing culture work.

### Complexity Transfer

- **Symptom:** A plan makes one part of the business cleaner or faster.
- **Disease:** It may be moving complexity into another team, user flow, customer segment, reporting process, or future maintenance burden.
- **Telltale signs:** One team celebrates simplicity while another expects more manual work, exceptions, or explanation burden.
- **Treatment:** Map who absorbs the complexity and decide whether that trade is intentional.

### Founder Exception Handling

- **Symptom:** The company works when a founder or senior leader personally intervenes.
- **Disease:** The operating system is not repeatable; exceptions are being solved through heroics instead of process, ownership, or product design.
- **Telltale signs:** Escalations route to the same person, special deals or customers bypass process, teams wait for founder judgment.
- **Treatment:** Turn recurring founder interventions into rules, decision rights, product changes, or accountable owners.

### Customer Distortion

- **Symptom:** A major customer, segment, or enterprise request appears to justify a roadmap shift.
- **Disease:** The company may be letting one buyer distort product direction, support burden, pricing, or positioning.
- **Telltale signs:** Custom work described as strategic, unclear repeatability, sales pressure outrunning product confidence, roadmap reshaped around one account.
- **Treatment:** Test whether the request represents a market pattern or a single-customer exception before committing.

## Workflow

1. **Capture the presenting symptom**
   - State the problem in the team's own words.
   - Note who is saying it and what they believe the fix is.

2. **Separate facts from interpretations**
   - List what is directly observed.
   - List what is inferred.
   - Mark missing evidence.

3. **Look for pattern matches**
   - Compare the situation against the pattern library.
   - Use more than one pattern if the situation is mixed.
   - Avoid overfitting when evidence is thin.

4. **Find the likely disease**
   - Ask what would explain the symptoms across teams, timelines, incentives, and repeated behavior.
   - Prefer operating causes over personality explanations unless the evidence supports a people issue.

5. **Expose hidden work and second-order consequences**
   - Identify work outside the visible project plan.
   - Include support, maintenance, migration, enablement, customer communication, measurement, cleanup, and decision load.

6. **Generate tests**
   - Create checks or questions that would confirm or disconfirm the diagnosis.
   - Include blunt internal questions and leadership-safe meeting versions.

7. **Recommend the smallest useful treatment**
   - Suggest the smallest intervention that reduces risk now.
   - Avoid prescribing a full reorg, reset, or strategy process unless the diagnosis requires it.

## Output Structure

Use this structure:

```md
# Treat the Disease, Not the Symptom

## Presenting Symptom
What the team says is wrong.

## Likely Disease
The deeper operating problem that may be driving the symptom.

## Diagnostic Evidence
Observed facts, inferred signals, and missing evidence.

## Pattern Match
Named patterns that fit and why.

## Hidden Work
Work likely missing from the plan.

## Second-Order Consequences
Problems this may create after the visible work ships.

## Severity Rating
Healthy, Watch, At Risk, Going Sideways, or Root Problem.

## Tests To Run
Checks that would confirm or disconfirm the diagnosis.

## Questions For The Next Meeting
### Blunt Internal
Private diagnostic questions.

### Meeting-Ready
Leadership-safe versions of the same questions.

## Treatment Plan
The smallest useful intervention, owner, and next step.

## Watchouts
What could make the condition worse.
```

## Sanitized Illustrations

### Rebrand Under-Scope

If a rebrand is scheduled for two months and the plan centers on visual identity, copy, and launch day, the likely disease may be operating under-scope. The missing work may include product surfaces, customer comms, sales enablement, legal review, internal language changes, support scripts, analytics updates, and old-brand cleanup.

### Second Product Drag

If a team launches a second product before the first product has a mature support and maintenance model, the likely disease may be portfolio sprawl. The new product can split roadmap focus, confuse packaging, increase support load, and force the same team to carry two operating models.

### Strategy Theater

If a strategy sounds clear but every old priority remains active, the likely disease may be lack of real tradeoffs. The symptom is not communication quality. The issue is that the strategy has not changed resource allocation, sequencing, or stopped work.

## Quality Bar

A good diagnostic memo:

- Names the stated symptom without accepting it at face value.
- Grounds the deeper diagnosis in evidence.
- Uses pattern recognition without forcing a pattern.
- Identifies hidden work and second-order consequences.
- Produces questions a leader can actually ask.
- Recommends a practical intervention, not a generic transformation.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Over-diagnosing:** do not claim a deep disease when the evidence only supports normal execution risk.
- **Blaming culture too quickly:** culture symptoms often come from operating design, incentives, overload, or broken trust.
- **Ignoring downstream teams:** many "simple" plans create invisible load elsewhere.
- **Skipping tests:** a good diagnosis should be falsifiable.
- **Making the metaphor cute:** keep the medical language useful and restrained.
